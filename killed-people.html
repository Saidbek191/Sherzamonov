Crackdown in Gorno-Badakhshan: Systematic Repression of the Pamiri Minority in Tajikistan

Mass Protests, Violent Suppression, and Ethnic Targeting in the Pamir Mountains At least 25 civilians were killed on May 18, 2022, when Tajik security forces opened fire on unarmed protesters in Khorog, the capital of the Gorno-Badakhshan Autonomous Region (GBAO). The protest, initiated peacefully, escalated into a deadly confrontation when government troops reportedly used rubber bullets, stun grenades, and tear gas against demonstrators who were marching to demand justice and accountability.

Background: The Roots of the Uprising

The latest violence follows years of simmering tensions between the central government of Tajikistan and the Pamiri people, a distinct ethnic and religious minority in the mountainous east of the country. The immediate cause of the unrest traces back to the killing of Gulbiddin Ziyobekov, a young Pamiri man shot by security forces in November 2021. His death triggered widespread protests in Khorog, during which three more protesters were killed and 17 wounded.
In response, hundreds of locals demanded the dismissal of the regional governor and an investigation into the killings. However, the government's reaction was militarized and punitive. Authorities launched a so-called “anti-terror operation” targeting activists, community leaders, and anyone linked to the demonstrations.

May 2022 Protest and Massacre

The May 2022 protests erupted after continued government refusal to meet public demands. Witnesses reported that several hundred people gathered in Khorog's main square. As the march advanced, security forces blocked roads and launched a violent assault. According to human rights groups and eyewitnesses:

  • At least 25 civilians were killed during the confrontation.
  • Dozens more were wounded.
  • Internet access in the region was cut off.
  • Roads in and out of GBAO were sealed, creating an information blackout.

Tajik authorities labeled the demonstrators as part of “criminal and terrorist groups,” justifying the use of military force.

Militarization and Special Operations

Following the initial crackdown, the government conducted a month-long "special anti-terror operation" throughout GBAO. Military forces, including snipers and armored vehicles, were deployed to the Rushan and Khorog districts. According to UN special rapporteurs, up to 40 people may have been killed in Rushan alone.
This militarized response was seen by many as an attempt to eliminate local leaders and silence dissent. Among those killed were:

  • Mamadboqir Mamadboqirov, a respected former military officer and community leader.
  • Zoir Rajabov and Khursand Mazorov, other prominent local figures.

All were labeled by the government as leaders of “criminal groups.”

Targeted Arrests and Closed Trials

The government arrested over 200 individuals in the aftermath of the protests, including at least 90 civil society activists, journalists, and lawyers. Many were subjected to closed trials, with no public access, no defense attorneys, and no transparency.
Notable detainees include:

  • Manuchehr Kholiqnazarov, head of the Lawyers’ Association in Pamir.
  • Faromuz Irgashov, a lawyer and head of “Commission 44,” a group created to investigate the November killings
  • Ulfatkhonim Mamadshoeva, a journalist accused of organizing the protests, now facing a potential 25-year prison sentence.
  • Kholbash Kholbashov, Mamadshoeva’s former husband, is facing a life sentence.

Some of the accused were reportedly abducted from Russia and forcibly returned to Tajikistan. Among them were Oraz Vazirbekov and Ramzi Vazirbekov, who disappeared from Moscow’s Domodedovo Airport and later appeared in state-controlled TV videos claiming they returned voluntarily — a narrative widely disputed by rights organizations.

Historical Context: A Legacy of Ethnic Persecution

The Pamiri population has long been marginalized by the Tajik state. During the 1992–1997 civil war, thousands of Pamiris were killed and displaced in what some activists describe as ethnic cleansing. Their language, culture, and Ismaili Shia faith differentiate them from the Sunni Tajik majority.
Periodic uprisings in 2012 and 2018 were met with similar repression, but the 2022 crackdown represents the most extensive and deadly campaign in recent history.

International Condemnation and Calls for Accountability

Human rights groups and international organizations, including Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, Genocide Watch, and the European Parliament, have condemned the Tajik government’s actions:

  • The European Parliament passed a resolution in July 2022 demanding an independent investigation into the violence and the immediate release of arbitrarily detained activists.
  • Genocide Watch issued a “Genocide Emergency” alert for Tajikistan, warning of a coordinated campaign of surveillance, detention, and persecution of Pamiris.
  • The United Nations has also raised alarms about the excessive use of force and denial of due process.

Conclusion: A Call for Justice and Reform

The Tajik government’s heavy-handed response in GBAO underscores a troubling trend: the systematic targeting of ethnic minorities under the guise of counterterrorism. The lack of transparency, mass arrests, and extrajudicial killings reflect a serious erosion of human rights and democratic accountability in Tajikistan.
As calls for justice grow louder, the international community faces a pressing question: Will global powers act to hold the Rahmon regime accountable, or will the plight of the Pamiri people continue to be ignored?
